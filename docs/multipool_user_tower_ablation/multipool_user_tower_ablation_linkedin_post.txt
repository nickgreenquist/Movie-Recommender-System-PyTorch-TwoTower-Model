Recency Is All You Need: A 12-Model Experiment on User-History Representation for Two-Tower Retrieval in Recommenders.

The standard way to build a user's vector in two-tower recommenders (pool the whole interaction history into one embedding (YouTube, 2016)) throws away the single most predictive signal.

I swapped the user tower of my movie recommender 12 ways, each differing only in which slices of history feed the user vector: whole history, liked/disliked movies, rating-weighted movies, or just the last movie watched. Same architecture and training (MovieLens 32M).

Three findings (none expected):

1. Adding the most-recently-watched movie lifted MRR +22%. One recent item nearly matched the ENTIRE summed history.

2. Valence doesn't help. The liked/disliked/rating-weighted channels behind a 4-pool tower add nothing over plain sum pool.

3. The drop-off past that one item is exactly why the field moved from pooling to Transformers (FMC → FPMC → SASRec).
